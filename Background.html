Background



The mass production of plastics dates back to 1950. If current production and waste management trends continue, plastic waste in landfills or in the natural environment will reach about 1.2 trillion tons by 2050. Today, plastic pollution has become a universal and urgent global concern. Among them, the size of microplastics is between 1 Î¼ m and 5 mm, which can be transmitted through wind, water and animals, and accumulate in the ocean, freshwater, soil and air, posing a harm to aquatic, Marine, soil organisms, ecosystems and human health. Economically, it will lead to lower fishing and tourism values and higher clean-up costs. It will also lead to the depletion of non-renewable resources and greenhouse gas emissions. Therefore, the study of how to effectively identify and treat the microplastics in the environment is of great significance.
